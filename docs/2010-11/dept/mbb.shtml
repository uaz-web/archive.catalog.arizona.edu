The School:

The School of Mind, Brain and Behavior is in the College of Science. The school represents a multidisciplinary group of departments and programs dedicated to the teaching and discovery of principles and mechanisms underlying minds, brains, and behaviors. The school was formed to foster interdisciplinary dialog and collaboration among students and faculty and to create new educational and research opportunities unconstrained by traditional disciplinary boundaries.

The Baccalaureate Program:

The Bachelor of Science with a major in Neuroscience and Cognitive Science consists of rigorous coursework that prepares students to enter graduate or professional school in one of several disciplines (e.g., speech and hearing, psychology, neuroscience, cognitive science, medicine, pharmacology, nursing, veterinary science), to be competitive for entry-level positions in the pharmaceutical and health care industries (or industries using medically or biologically related technologies), to be a high school science teacher, or to work in non-profit health organizations.  The program has a broad science foundation and core courses that expose students to the concepts and methods of scientific inquiry in neuroscience and cognitive science. Students also choose an area of emphasis based on their interests:   Communication Science, Neurobiology, Neuroscience of Aging, Computational Neuroscience, or a thematic area.   In addition to traditional courses, students are encouraged to do an internship with one of the Neuroscience community partners in order to get experience in a clinical or research setting. 

 

Interested students should declare the Pre-Neuroscience and Cognitive Science Major.  Admission to the degree program from the pre-major is competitive and based on academic performance in prerequisite courses (a minimum 2.500 GPA in the science prerequisites, and a grade of B in the MBB 200-level introductory course).  Students should apply during their sophomore year for admission to the major in their junior year.
