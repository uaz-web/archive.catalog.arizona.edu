The School:

The School of Anthropology is in the College of Social and Behavioral Sciences.

The science of anthropology is the study of human beings, their origins, thought, and behavior. The School of Anthropology offers graduate and undergraduate course work in four subdisciplines: archaeology, physical anthropology, cultural anthropology and linguistic anthropology; as well as specialized training for field research. Special internships and independent studies in museology are available through the Arizona State Museum. The Bureau of Applied Research in Anthropology is a center for applied anthropological research in the American Southwest and similar multicultural and ecological settings elsewhere in the world. The school cooperates with the Arizona Health Sciences Center in offering a program in medical anthropology.

Anthropology BA:

The Bachelor of Arts program gives students a broad overview of the discipline: archaeology, physical and cultural anthropology, and linguistic anthropology.  Students first take a common set of courses which provide basic training in all four subdisciplines. The student may then select one of three programs: (1) a general program requiring upper-division work in each of the four subdisciplines; (2) a special program that requires upper-division course work in each of two subdisciplines; or (3) a topical or a real specialization approved by the undergraduate advisor.

Anthropology BS:

The Bachelor of Science program focuses on tracks in Archaeological Science and Human Biology which span the social, behavioral, natural and physical sciences. The two tracks draw on a variety of methods and theories from the sciences to provide insights on the evolution and nature of global human diversity. The BS provides 1) an undergraduate education that will make students more competitive for future graduate study in the social and natural sciences; 2) a wide range of undergraduate research opportunities; and 3) specialized training that increases employment opportunities in applied fields (e.g., in cultural resources management and the health professions).
